About TSA Tracker

The only free tool that shows live TSA checkpoint wait times pulled directly from official airport systems — not crowdsourced, not estimated, not stale.

Why We Built This

Getting to the airport is stressful enough. But even after you arrive, the question of how long is the TSA line right now? is surprisingly hard to answer. Most apps guess. Some aggregate Twitter posts. A few use historical averages and call it "live."

TSA Tracker started from a simple frustration: we wanted to know the actual wait time at the checkpoint before heading to security. Not a model. Not a community report from 40 minutes ago. The real number, right now.

It turns out most major airports publish that data — they just don't make it easy to find. TSA Tracker finds it, normalizes it, and puts it in front of you within 2 minutes of when the airport updates it.

How the Data Works

Each supported airport publishes its checkpoint wait time data through its own system — some via public JSON APIs, some through embedded data feeds on their websites. TSA Tracker polls these sources approximately every 2 minutes, parses the raw data, and stores a time-stamped snapshot for each checkpoint and lane type.

When you open the site, you're seeing the most recent snapshot — usually no more than 2 minutes old. The timestamp shown on each checkpoint card tells you exactly when that reading was taken, so you always know how fresh the data is.

The 12-hour history chart shows every snapshot we've collected during that window, so you can see patterns: when does the 7 AM rush peak? When does the afternoon lull hit? That context helps you decide when to leave for the airport, not just whether lines are long right now.

What "Official Airport Systems" Means

Each airport's authority or management company collects wait time data from checkpoint sensors, staffing systems, or queue-monitoring cameras. They use this data internally for operational planning and often publish it on their website for travelers. That is the data TSA Tracker reads.

This is meaningfully different from crowdsourced reports (where someone at the airport posts a rough estimate) or algorithmic predictions (where a model guesses based on historical patterns and flight schedules). Official airport data reflects what sensors and staff are actually measuring at that moment.

Not every airport publishes this data publicly. Some systems are behind API keys or require partnerships. That's why our list of live airports is limited — we only show real data, not fabricated numbers.

Lane Types Explained

Where airports provide lane-level data, TSA Tracker shows separate wait times for each lane type:

  • Standard — the regular TSA security line open to all passengers.
  • TSA PreCheck — expedited screening for enrolled travelers. Shoes stay on, laptops stay in bags. PreCheck lines are almost always shorter than standard, often dramatically so during peak hours.
  • CLEAR — a paid biometric identity verification service that can skip the ID-check step. CLEAR members still go through TSA screening, but the identity portion moves faster.

Not all airports report separate wait times by lane. Some only publish a single aggregate figure for the whole checkpoint. In those cases, we display the single available number.

Color Coding Guide

Each checkpoint card uses a simple color system to help you immediately understand conditions:

  • Green — short wait, generally under 15 minutes. You're in good shape.
  • Yellow — moderate wait, roughly 15–30 minutes. Factor this into your timing.
  • Red — long wait, over 30 minutes. Arrive earlier than you planned, or check a different checkpoint if your airport has multiple security entrances.

Tips for Getting Through Security Faster

  • Check the history chart before you leave home. If you're flying at 8 AM and the chart shows consistent red between 6–9 AM, leave earlier or prepare for a long line.
  • Consider TSA PreCheck. The enrollment fee pays for itself the first time you walk through a 3-minute PreCheck line while the standard line is 45 minutes long. Enrollment is $85 for five years.
  • Know your airport's checkpoints. Large airports like ORD, LAX, and JFK have multiple security checkpoints in different terminals. One might have a long line while another is clear. Check your terminal before committing to a checkpoint.
  • Pack smart. The biggest cause of slow screening (beyond volume) is bags that need secondary inspection. Keep liquids accessible, laptops easy to pull out, and avoid wearing heavy metal accessories.
  • TSA recommends 2 hours for domestic, 3 for international. That's a minimum — if wait times are high during your window, add more buffer.

Accuracy and Limitations

TSA Tracker is as accurate as the official data each airport publishes. When airports update their feeds promptly (most do), our data is very accurate. However, a few caveats are worth knowing:

  • Some airports update their feeds less frequently than others. Where we can detect stale data, we flag it.
  • Wait times can change rapidly during peak periods. A 5-minute wait can become 25 minutes in the time it takes you to park and get to the terminal.
  • TSA Tracker shows data per checkpoint, not a prediction of what wait times will be when you arrive. Use the history chart for forward-looking planning.
  • A small number of airports use estimated or sensor-averaged data rather than real-time queue measurement. We note this where we're aware of it.

Is TSA Tracker Free?

Yes, TSA Tracker is completely free to use. The site is supported by advertising. We show clearly labeled ads in the ad slots on the page — that's it. We don't charge for access, don't require sign-ups, and don't sell data.
